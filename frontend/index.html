How vectors multiply. Dot product for parallel projection, Cross product for perpendicular rotation.

Comprehensive Mathematics

Work: A Physical Derivation of the Dot Product

Consider a box being pulled across the floor by a force \(\vec{F}\) acting at an angle \(\theta\) relative to the displacement \(\vec{d}\).

Physics dictates that only the component of force acting in the direction of motion contributes to Work. From the figure, we drop a perpendicular from the tip of \(\vec{F}\) to \(\vec{d}\), forming a right triangle. The horizontal component of the force is \(F \cos\theta\).

Therefore, the total Work \(W\) is the product of this horizontal force and the displacement distance \(d\):

$$W = (F \cos\theta)d = F d \cos\theta$$

This scalar quantity—multiplying parallel components—is formally defined as the Dot Product of the two vectors:

$$\vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} = |\vec{F}| |\vec{d}| \cos\theta$$

Torque: A Physical Derivation of the Cross Product

Imagine using a wrench to turn a bolt. The wrench represents the lever arm vector \(\vec{r}\), and you apply a force \(\vec{F}\) at an angle \(\theta\).

To maximize the turning effect (Torque), you want to pull perpendicular to the wrench. From our figure, the component of force perpendicular to the lever arm is \(F \sin\theta\).

The magnitude of the Torque \(\vec{\tau}\) is the lever arm length multiplied by this perpendicular force component:

$$|\vec{\tau}| = |\vec{r}| (|\vec{F}| \sin\theta) = |\vec{r}| |\vec{F}| \sin\theta$$

This operation—multiplying perpendicular components to generate an orthogonal rotational axis—is formally defined as the Cross Product:

$$\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$$

Physical Derivation of the Parallelogram Law

Imagine a heavy crate being pushed by two people. Person A pushes with Force \(\vec{A}\) and Person B pushes with Force \(\vec{B}\) at an angle \(\theta\). The crate moves along the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by these two forces, representing the net force (Resultant \(\vec{R}\)).

To find the exact magnitude of \(\vec{R}\), we extend the line of action of \(\vec{A}\) and drop a perpendicular from the tip of the parallelogram. This forms a large right-angled triangle (as seen by the dotted lines in the figure).

The base of this large triangle is \(|\vec{A}| + |\vec{B}|\cos\theta\), and the height is \(|\vec{B}|\sin\theta\). Applying the Pythagorean theorem to this large triangle:

$$|\vec{R}|^2 = (|\vec{A}| + |\vec{B}|\cos\theta)^2 + (|\vec{B}|\sin\theta)^2$$
$$|\vec{R}|^2 = |\vec{A}|^2 + 2|\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos\theta + |\vec{B}|^2\cos^2\theta + |\vec{B}|^2\sin^2\theta$$

Using the trigonometric identity \(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\), we arrive at the exact magnitude of the resultant vector:

$$\boxed{|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{|\vec{A}|^2 + |\vec{B}|^2 + 2|\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos\theta}}$$

Physical Derivation of Vector Subtraction

Suppose you want to find the relative force between \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), which is \(\vec{A} - \vec{B}\). Subtracting a vector is identical to adding its opposite: \(\vec{A} + (-\vec{B})\).

As shown in the figure, reversing the direction of \(\vec{B}\) creates \(-\vec{B}\). The angle between \(\vec{A}\) and \(-\vec{B}\) is exactly \((180^\circ - \theta)\).

If we apply our parallelogram formula derived earlier, substituting \((180^\circ - \theta)\) for the angle:

$$|\vec{A}-\vec{B}| = \sqrt{|\vec{A}|^2 + |\vec{B}|^2 + 2|\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos(180^\circ - \theta)}$$

Since \(\cos(180^\circ - \theta) = -\cos\theta\), the formula simplifies perfectly to:

$$\boxed{|\vec{A}-\vec{B}| = \sqrt{|\vec{A}|^2 + |\vec{B}|^2 - 2|\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos\theta}}$$

Physical Derivation of the Kinematic Equations

Consider a car moving on a straight road. It starts at time \(t=0\) with an initial velocity \(v_0\) and accelerates at a constant rate \(a\). After time \(t\), it reaches a final velocity \(v\).

1. The Velocity Equation

Acceleration is physically defined as the rate at which velocity changes: \(a = \frac{v - v_0}{t}\). This can be seen as the slope in the Velocity-Time graph below.

Rearranging this directly gives us the first kinematic equation:

$$\boxed{v = v_0 + at}$$

2. The Displacement Equation

Because the acceleration is constant, the velocity increases steadily. The distance travelled is the area under the Velocity-Time graph, which can be split into a rectangle and a triangle.

Total displacement \(x\) is just average velocity multiplied by time:

$$x = \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right) t$$

Substitute the velocity equation \(v = v_0 + at\) into this expression:

$$x = \left(\frac{v_0 + (v_0 + at)}{2}\right) t = \left(\frac{2v_0 + at}{2}\right) t$$

Distributing the \(t\) yields the famous displacement formula:

$$\boxed{x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2}$$

Plotting this displacement against time yields a parabolic curve (since position depends on \(t^2\)):

3. The Time-Independent Equation

Instead of splitting the area, we can also calculate the total displacement as the area of the single trapezium OABC.

If we want to relate velocity to position without caring about how much time has passed, we solve the first equation for time: \(t = \frac{v - v_0}{a}\). We then substitute this into our average velocity formula \(x = \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right) t\):

$$x = \left(\frac{v + v_0}{2}\right) \left(\frac{v - v_0}{a}\right)$$

The numerators form a difference of squares \((v + v_0)(v - v_0) = v^2 - v_0^2\):

$$2ax = v^2 - v_0^2$$
$$\boxed{v^2 = v_0^2 + 2ax}$$

Plotting velocity squared (\(v^2\)) against position (\(x\)) yields a linear relationship with a slope of \(2a\):

1. Hamiltonian Formulation (Energy)

Consider a particle of mass \(m\) under a constant force \(F = ma\). The corresponding potential energy is \(V(x) = -max\). The kinetic energy is \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2\). The Hamiltonian \(\mathcal{H}\) (total mechanical energy) is:

$$\mathcal{H} = T + V = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - max$$

The principle of conservation of energy demands \(\frac{d\mathcal{H}}{dt} = 0\):

$$\frac{d\mathcal{H}}{dt} = m\dot{x}\ddot{x} - ma\dot{x} = 0 \implies \boxed{\ddot{x} = a}$$

2. Lagrangian Formulation

The Lagrangian is defined as \(L = T - V\):

$$L = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + max$$

Applying the Euler-Lagrange equation \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = 0\):

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}} = m\dot{x} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(m\dot{x}) = m\ddot{x}$$
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = ma$$

Substituting back yields the exact same equation of motion:

$$m\ddot{x} - ma = 0 \implies \boxed{\ddot{x} = a}$$

Comprehensive Mathematics

Physical Derivation of Projectile Motion

Imagine a cannon firing a cannonball at an angle \(\theta\) with initial speed \(v_0\). Because gravity acts purely downwards, the motion cleanly separates into independent horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components.

1. The Independent Axes

Horizontally, there is no air resistance or force, so the velocity is constant: \(v_x = v_0 \cos\theta\).

Vertically, gravity pulls the ball down: \(v_y = v_0 \sin\theta - gt\).

By integrating these over time, we get the exact position of the cannonball at any time \(t\):

$$x(t) = (v_0\cos\theta)t \quad \text{and} \quad y(t) = (v_0\sin\theta)t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

2. Peak Height (H)

Looking at the figure, at the absolute peak of the trajectory, the cannonball stops moving up and is purely moving horizontally. Therefore, \(v_y = 0\).

Setting \(v_0 \sin\theta - gt = 0\) gives the time to peak: \(t_{\text{peak}} = \frac{v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\). Substituting this into the \(y(t)\) equation yields the maximum height \(H\):

$$\boxed{H = \frac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}}$$

3. Horizontal Range (R)

The total time the cannonball is in the air is twice the peak time: \(T_{\text{total}} = \frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\). To find how far it travels horizontally (Range), we plug this total time into the constant-velocity \(x(t)\) equation:

$$R = (v_0\cos\theta) \left(\frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\right) = \frac{v_0^2 (2\sin\theta\cos\theta)}{g}$$

Using the trigonometric identity \(2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin(2\theta)\), we derive the final Range formula:

$$\boxed{R = \frac{v_0^2\sin 2\theta}{g}}$$

1. Hamiltonian Formulation (Energy)

Let the coordinates be \((x, y)\). The Hamiltonian \(\mathcal{H} = T + V\) is:

$$\mathcal{H} = \frac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) + mgy$$

Hamilton's equations state \(\dot{p}_x = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial x}\). Since \(\mathcal{H}\) has no dependence on \(x\):

$$\dot{p}_x = 0 \implies p_x = m\dot{x} = \text{const}$$

2. Lagrangian Formulation & Noether's Theorem

The Lagrangian \(L = T - V\) is:

$$L = \frac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) - mgy$$

Since \(L\) does not explicitly contain \(x\) (\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = 0\)), the coordinate \(x\) is cyclic.

By Noether's Theorem, the conjugate momentum \(p_x\) is perfectly conserved:

$$p_x = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}} = m\dot{x} = \text{const} \implies \ddot{x} = 0$$

This instantly proves that horizontal velocity is constant throughout the flight!

The Mathematics of Arialsection

When two objects move along the same axis, an "overtake" or collision occurs mathematically at the exact moment \(t\) when their position functions are equal: \(x_A(t) = x_B(t)\).

$$ x_{0A} + v_{0A}t + \frac{1}{2}a_At^2 = x_{0B} + v_{0B}t + \frac{1}{2}a_Bt^2 $$

By rearranging terms, we form a standard quadratic equation in time \(t\):

$$ \frac{1}{2}(a_A - a_B)t^2 + (v_{0A} - v_{0B})t + (x_{0A} - x_{0B}) = 0 $$

Solving this quadratic equation using the quadratic formula yields the exact time(s) of intersection. On the Position-Time graph, this corresponds to the visual crossing point of the two parabolas!

JEE Projectile Motion Concepts

Fundamental Projectile Motion Equations (Level Ground)

For a projectile launched with speed \(v_0\) at angle \(\theta\) from a flat surface, with gravitational acceleration \(g\):

1. Parametric Equations of Motion

$$x(t) = (v_0 \cos\theta)\,t$$
$$y(t) = (v_0 \sin\theta)\,t - \tfrac{1}{2}g\,t^2$$

2. Time of Flight

Setting \(y(T) = 0\) and solving for the non-zero root:

$$\boxed{T = \frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}}$$

3. Horizontal Range

Substituting \(T\) into \(x(t)\) and using the identity \(2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin 2\theta\):

$$\boxed{R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}}$$

4. Maximum Height

At the peak, \(v_y = 0\). The time to reach peak is \(t_p = \frac{v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\). Substituting:

$$\boxed{H_{\max} = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2\theta}{2g}}$$

5. Equation of Trajectory

Eliminating \(t\) from the parametric equations:

$$\boxed{y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g\,x^2}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}}$$

The Complementary Angle Theorem

This is one of the most important and frequently tested concepts in JEE. For the same launch speed \(v_0\), if two projectiles are launched at complementary angles \(\theta\) and \((90^\circ - \theta)\), they will have the same horizontal range.

Proof

Consider the range formula \(R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}\). Now substitute \(\theta' = 90^\circ - \theta\):

$$R' = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2(90^\circ - \theta)}{g} = \frac{v_0^2 \sin(180^\circ - 2\theta)}{g}$$

Using the identity \(\sin(180^\circ - x) = \sin x\):

$$\boxed{R' = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} = R}$$

Hence proved: \(R(\theta) = R(90^\circ - \theta)\). Try this in the simulation above! Set P1 to 30° and P3 to 60° with the same speed—they land at the exact same spot!

But Their Heights and Times Are Different!

Even though the range is identical, the two trajectories are dramatically different:

$$\frac{H_1}{H_2} = \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\sin^2(90^\circ - \theta)} = \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \tan^2\theta$$
$$\frac{T_1}{T_2} = \frac{\sin\theta}{\sin(90^\circ - \theta)} = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \tan\theta$$

The steeper angle always goes higher and stays in the air longer, while the shallow angle reaches the target faster. This is exactly what you observe in the simulation!

JEE Advanced: Key Insights

1. Maximum Range Condition

Since \(R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}\) and \(\sin 2\theta \leq 1\), the maximum value occurs when \(\sin 2\theta = 1\), i.e., \(2\theta = 90^\circ\):

$$\boxed{R_{\max} = \frac{v_0^2}{g} \quad \text{at } \theta = 45^\circ}$$

At \(45^\circ\), the maximum height is exactly \(\frac{R_{\max}}{4}\). Verify this in the readout table above!

2. Relation Between R and H

For any angle \(\theta\), the range and height are connected by:

$$\boxed{R = 4H_{\max}\cot\theta}$$

3. Speed at Any Height

Using energy conservation (no air resistance), the speed at height \(h\) is independent of angle:

$$\boxed{v = \sqrt{v_0^2 - 2gh}}$$

This means all projectiles launched at the same speed pass through the same height with the same speed!

Boat & River Mathematics

Physical Derivation of River Crossing

When a boat crosses a flowing river, it experiences two independent velocities simultaneously: the velocity of the boat relative to the water (\(\vec{v}_{\text{boat}}\)) and the velocity of the water relative to the shore (\(\vec{v}_{\text{river}}\)).

The boat's true path, as seen from the shore (\(\vec{v}_{\text{net}}\)), is the vector sum of these two velocities. From the geometric triangle in the figure:

$$\vec{v}_{\text{net}} = \vec{v}_{\text{boat}} + \vec{v}_{\text{river}}$$

1. Crossing Time

To find the time it takes to cross a river of width \(W\), we only look at the velocity component straight across the river (the Y-axis). The river's current acts strictly horizontally (X-axis) and does not help or hinder the crossing.

The boat's vertical velocity component is \(v_{\text{boat}} \cos\theta\). Using \(\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Velocity}}\), the exact time to cross is:

$$\boxed{t = \frac{W}{v_{\text{boat}} \cos\theta}}$$

2. Downstream Drift

How far downstream does the boat land? This horizontal displacement (Drift) depends entirely on the boat's horizontal velocity component and the time \(t\).

The total horizontal velocity is the sum of the river's speed and the boat's own horizontal component: \(v_x = v_{\text{river}} + v_{\text{boat}} \sin\theta\).

Multiplying this by the time derived above gives the Drift:

$$\boxed{\text{Drift} = (v_{\text{river}} + v_{\text{boat}} \sin\theta) \cdot t}$$

3. Zero Drift (Straight Path)

If we want to land exactly straight across (\(\text{Drift} = 0\)), we must cancel out the river's flow. This means our horizontal velocity component must be exactly equal and opposite to the river's velocity: \(v_{\text{boat}}\sin\theta = -v_{\text{river}}\).

$$\boxed{\sin\theta = \frac{-v_{\text{river}}}{v_{\text{boat}}}}$$

If the river is faster than the boat, this equation yields a number greater than 1, proving it is physically impossible to go straight across!

Car Chase Mathematics

Derivation of the Catch-up Time

Suppose Car A is chasing Car B. At \(t=0\), Car A is at \(x=0\) and Car B is at \(x = d\). Their positions over time are given by:

$$x_{\text{A}}(t) = v_{\text{A}} t$$
$$x_{\text{B}}(t) = d + v_{\text{B}} t$$

A "catch" occurs when their positions are exactly equal (\(x_{\text{A}} = x_{\text{B}}\)):

$$v_{\text{A}} t = d + v_{\text{B}} t \implies (v_{\text{A}} - v_{\text{B}}) t = d$$

This allows us to solve for the time it takes Car A to catch Car B:

$$\boxed{t = \frac{d}{v_{\text{A}} - v_{\text{B}}}}$$

Relative Velocity Perspective

Instead of watching from the ground, imagine you are sitting in Car B (the target). From your perspective, you are stationary (\(v_{\text{B}} = 0\)).

However, you see Car A approaching you at a relative velocity of \(v_{\text{rel}} = v_{\text{A}} - v_{\text{B}}\). The initial distance is \(d\).

Using the simple formula \(\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}\):

$$t = \frac{d}{v_{\text{rel}}} = \frac{d}{v_{\text{A}} - v_{\text{B}}}$$

Both the absolute frame and the relative frame yield the exact same physical result, proving the power of relative reference frames!

Rain & Umbrella Mathematics

Derivation of the Umbrella Angle

Suppose rain is falling straight down with velocity \(\vec{v}_{\text{rain}}\), and a person is walking forward with velocity \(\vec{v}_{\text{man}}\).

The relative velocity of the rain with respect to the moving person is given by vector subtraction:

$$\vec{v}_{\text{rel}} = \vec{v}_{\text{rain}} - \vec{v}_{\text{man}} = \vec{v}_{\text{rain}} + (-\vec{v}_{\text{man}})$$

1. Visualizing the Triangle

As shown in the right side of the figure, we add the downward rain vector (\(\vec{v}_{\text{rain}}\)) to the reversed person vector (\(-\vec{v}_{\text{man}}\)). This creates a right-angled triangle where the hypotenuse is the relative velocity \(\vec{v}_{\text{rel}}\).

2. The Apparent Speed

Using the Pythagorean theorem on this triangle, the speed at which the rain appears to hit the person is:

$$|\vec{v}_{\text{rel}}| = \sqrt{v_{\text{rain}}^2 + v_{\text{man}}^2}$$

This shows that running faster makes the rain hit you harder.

3. The Optimal Umbrella Angle

To stay dry, the person must tilt the umbrella to match the angle of the incoming relative rain. Looking at our triangle, the angle \(\theta\) (measured from the vertical) satisfies:

$$\tan\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}} = \frac{v_{\text{man}}}{v_{\text{rain}}}$$
$$\boxed{\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{v_{\text{man}}}{v_{\text{rain}}}\right)}$$

This mathematical result perfectly matches our intuition: the faster you run (\(v_{\text{man}}\) increases), the more forward you must tilt your umbrella!

Question 1

Ship A is located at the origin (0,0) moving due East with velocity 10 km/h. Ship B is located 100 km due South of A (0, -100) moving North-West (135°) with velocity \(10\sqrt{2}\) km/h. Find the shortest distance between them and the time taken.

Mathematical Solution

In Ground Frame, both ships move. In Ship A's frame, Ship A is stationary. The relative velocity of Ship B with respect to A is \( \vec{v}_{BA} = \vec{v}_B - \vec{v}_A \).

\( \vec{v}_A = 10 \hat{i} \)

\( \vec{v}_B = -10 \hat{i} + 10 \hat{j} \) (since \( 10\sqrt{2}\cos 135^\circ = -10 \))

\( \vec{v}_{BA} = (-10 - 10)\hat{i} + 10\hat{j} = -20\hat{i} + 10\hat{j} \)

The shortest distance \( d \) is the perpendicular dropped from A (origin) to the relative path of B. The relative path starts at (0, -100).

The angle of \(\vec{v}_{BA}\) with the negative x-axis is \(\alpha = \arctan(10/20) \approx 26.56^\circ\).

Shortest distance \( d_{\min} = 100 \cos(26.56^\circ) = 100 \times \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} = 40\sqrt{5} \approx 89.44 \text{ km} \).

Time \( t = \frac{\text{Distance along path}}{|\vec{v}_{BA}|} = \frac{100 \sin(26.56^\circ)}{\sqrt{20^2+10^2}} = \frac{100(1/\sqrt{5})}{10\sqrt{5}} = 2 \text{ hours} \).

Question 2

Particle 1 is projected from height H horizontally with speed u. Particle 2 is projected from the ground at horizontal distance D, aimed towards the tower with speed v at angle \(\theta\). If they collide, find the relation between H, D, v, \(\theta\).

Mathematical Solution

In a gravity-free relative frame, Particle 1 moves with constant velocity \(u \hat{i}\) and Particle 2 moves with \(-v\cos\theta \hat{i} + v\sin\theta \hat{j}\).

Relative displacement must exactly align with relative velocity for them to collide. The relative velocity of 2 w.r.t 1 is \(-(u + v\cos\theta)\hat{i} + v\sin\theta \hat{j}\).

Since Particle 2 starts at (D, 0) and Particle 1 is at (0, H), the time taken to cover the horizontal distance is \(t = \frac{D}{u + v\cos\theta}\).

The time to cover the vertical distance is \(t = \frac{H}{v\sin\theta}\).

For a collision, these times must be equal:

$$ \boxed{\frac{D}{u + v\cos\theta} = \frac{H}{v\sin\theta}} $$

Question 3

A ball is thrown with speed u at angle \(\theta\). Find the radius of curvature when its velocity makes angle \(\theta/2\) with the horizontal.

Mathematical Solution

The formula for radius of curvature is \(R = \frac{v^2}{a_{\perp}}\), where \(a_{\perp}\) is the component of acceleration normal to the velocity vector.

Since the only acceleration is gravity \(g\) acting downwards, when the velocity vector is at an angle \(\phi\) to the horizontal, \(a_{\perp} = g \cos\phi\).

Horizontal velocity is constant: \(v \cos\phi = u \cos\theta \implies v = \frac{u \cos\theta}{\cos\phi}\).

Therefore, \(R = \frac{(u \cos\theta / \cos\phi)^2}{g \cos\phi} = \frac{u^2 \cos^2\theta}{g \cos^3\phi}\).

Substituting \(\phi = \theta/2\):

$$\boxed{R = \frac{u^2 \cos^2\theta}{g \cos^3(\theta/2)}}$$

Question 4

A wedge (angle \(\alpha\)) rests on the ground. A particle is projected towards it with velocity u at angle \(\theta\). The wedge accelerates horizontally towards the particle at \(a\). If the particle strikes the wedge exactly perpendicularly, find the time of collision.

Mathematical Solution

In the wedge frame, the wedge is stationary. The particle experiences a "Pseudo-Gravity" due to the wedge's acceleration: \(\vec{g}_{\text{eff}} = \vec{g} - \vec{a}_{\text{wedge}}\).

Since the wedge accelerates towards the particle (leftwards, -x), the pseudo-force is rightwards (+x). Thus \(\vec{g}_{\text{eff}}\) has a downward component \(g\) and a rightward component \(a\).

We resolve the initial velocity and \(\vec{g}_{\text{eff}}\) along the wedge plane (x') and perpendicular to it (y'):

  • \(u_{x'} = u \cos(\theta - \alpha)\)
  • \(u_{y'} = u \sin(\theta - \alpha)\)
  • \(g_{\text{eff}, x'} = g \sin\alpha - a \cos\alpha\) (acting down the plane)
  • \(g_{\text{eff}, y'} = g \cos\alpha + a \sin\alpha\) (acting into the plane)

For a perpendicular strike, the velocity along the plane (\(v_{x'}\)) must be zero when it hits. The time of flight is determined by the y' motion: \(T = \frac{2 u_{y'}}{g_{\text{eff}, y'}}\).

$$v_{x'} = u_{x'} - g_{\text{eff}, x'} T = 0 \implies u_{x'} = g_{\text{eff}, x'} \left( \frac{2 u_{y'}}{g_{\text{eff}, y'}} \right)$$

Physical Derivation of the Kinematic Equations

Consider a car moving on a straight road. It starts at time \(t=0\) with an initial velocity \(v_0\) and accelerates at a constant rate \(a\). After time \(t\), it reaches a final velocity \(v\).

1. The Velocity Equation

Acceleration is physically defined as the rate at which velocity changes: \(a = \frac{v - v_0}{t}\). This can be seen as the slope in the Velocity-Time graph below.

Rearranging this directly gives us the first kinematic equation:

$$\boxed{v = v_0 + at}$$

2. The Displacement Equation

Because the acceleration is constant, the velocity increases steadily. The distance travelled is the area under the Velocity-Time graph, which can be split into a rectangle and a triangle.

Total displacement \(x\) is just average velocity multiplied by time:

$$x = \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right) t$$

Substitute the velocity equation \(v = v_0 + at\) into this expression:

$$x = \left(\frac{v_0 + (v_0 + at)}{2}\right) t = \left(\frac{2v_0 + at}{2}\right) t$$

Distributing the \(t\) yields the famous displacement formula:

$$\boxed{x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2}$$

3. The Time-Independent Equation

Instead of splitting the area, we can also calculate the total displacement as the area of the single trapezium OABC.

If we want to relate velocity to position without caring about how much time has passed, we solve the first equation for time: \(t = \frac{v - v_0}{a}\). We then substitute this into our average velocity formula \(x = \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right) t\):

$$x = \left(\frac{v + v_0}{2}\right) \left(\frac{v - v_0}{a}\right)$$

The numerators form a difference of squares \((v + v_0)(v - v_0) = v^2 - v_0^2\):

$$2ax = v^2 - v_0^2$$
$$\boxed{v^2 = v_0^2 + 2ax}$$

1. Hamiltonian Formulation (Energy)

Consider a particle of mass \(m\) under a constant force \(F = ma\). The corresponding potential energy is \(V(x) = -max\). The kinetic energy is \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2\). The Hamiltonian \(\mathcal{H}\) (total mechanical energy) is:

$$\mathcal{H} = T + V = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - max$$

The principle of conservation of energy demands \(\frac{d\mathcal{H}}{dt} = 0\):

$$\frac{d\mathcal{H}}{dt} = m\dot{x}\ddot{x} - ma\dot{x} = 0 \implies \boxed{\ddot{x} = a}$$

2. Lagrangian Formulation

The Lagrangian is defined as \(L = T - V\):

$$L = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + max$$

Applying the Euler-Lagrange equation \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = 0\):

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}} = m\dot{x} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(m\dot{x}) = m\ddot{x}$$
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = ma$$

Substituting back yields the exact same equation of motion:

$$m\ddot{x} - ma = 0 \implies \boxed{\ddot{x} = a}$$

Physical Derivation of Projectile Motion

Imagine a cannon firing a cannonball at an angle \(\theta\) with initial speed \(v_0\). Because gravity acts purely downwards, the motion cleanly separates into independent horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components.

1. The Independent Axes

Horizontally, there is no air resistance or force, so the velocity is constant: \(v_x = v_0 \cos\theta\).

Vertically, gravity pulls the ball down: \(v_y = v_0 \sin\theta - gt\).

By integrating these over time, we get the exact position of the cannonball at any time \(t\):

$$x(t) = (v_0\cos\theta)t \quad \text{and} \quad y(t) = (v_0\sin\theta)t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

2. Peak Height (H)

Looking at the figure, at the absolute peak of the trajectory, the cannonball stops moving up and is purely moving horizontally. Therefore, \(v_y = 0\).

Setting \(v_0 \sin\theta - gt = 0\) gives the time to peak: \(t_{\text{peak}} = \frac{v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\). Substituting this into the \(y(t)\) equation yields the maximum height \(H\):

$$\boxed{H = \frac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}}$$

3. Horizontal Range (R)

The total time the cannonball is in the air is twice the peak time: \(T_{\text{total}} = \frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\). To find how far it travels horizontally (Range), we plug this total time into the constant-velocity \(x(t)\) equation:

$$R = (v_0\cos\theta) \left(\frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\right) = \frac{v_0^2 (2\sin\theta\cos\theta)}{g}$$

Using the trigonometric identity \(2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin(2\theta)\), we derive the final Range formula:

$$\boxed{R = \frac{v_0^2\sin 2\theta}{g}}$$

1. Hamiltonian Formulation (Energy)

Let the coordinates be \((x, y)\). The Hamiltonian \(\mathcal{H} = T + V\) is:

$$\mathcal{H} = \frac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) + mgy$$

Hamilton's equations state \(\dot{p}_x = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial x}\). Since \(\mathcal{H}\) has no dependence on \(x\):

$$\dot{p}_x = 0 \implies p_x = m\dot{x} = \text{const}$$

2. Lagrangian Formulation & Noether's Theorem

The Lagrangian \(L = T - V\) is:

$$L = \frac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) - mgy$$

Since \(L\) does not explicitly contain \(x\) (\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = 0\)), the coordinate \(x\) is cyclic.

By Noether's Theorem, the conjugate momentum \(p_x\) is perfectly conserved:

$$p_x = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}} = m\dot{x} = \text{const} \implies \ddot{x} = 0$$

This instantly proves that horizontal velocity is constant throughout the flight!

This is one of the most important and frequently tested concepts in JEE. For the same launch speed \(v_0\), if two projectiles are launched at complementary angles \(\theta\) and \((90^\circ - \theta)\), they will have the same horizontal range.

Physical Derivation of River Crossing

When a boat crosses a flowing river, it experiences two independent velocities simultaneously: the velocity of the boat relative to the water (\(\vec{v}_{\text{boat}}\)) and the velocity of the water relative to the shore (\(\vec{v}_{\text{river}}\)).

The boat's true path, as seen from the shore (\(\vec{v}_{\text{net}}\)), is the vector sum of these two velocities. From the geometric triangle in the figure:

$$\vec{v}_{\text{net}} = \vec{v}_{\text{boat}} + \vec{v}_{\text{river}}$$

1. Crossing Time

To find the time it takes to cross a river of width \(W\), we only look at the velocity component straight across the river (the Y-axis). The river's current acts strictly horizontally (X-axis) and does not help or hinder the crossing.

The boat's vertical velocity component is \(v_{\text{boat}} \cos\theta\). Using \(\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Velocity}}\), the exact time to cross is:

$$\boxed{t = \frac{W}{v_{\text{boat}} \cos\theta}}$$

2. Downstream Drift

How far downstream does the boat land? This horizontal displacement (Drift) depends entirely on the boat's horizontal velocity component and the time \(t\).

The total horizontal velocity is the sum of the river's speed and the boat's own horizontal component: \(v_x = v_{\text{river}} + v_{\text{boat}} \sin\theta\).

Multiplying this by the time derived above gives the Drift:

$$\boxed{\text{Drift} = (v_{\text{river}} + v_{\text{boat}} \sin\theta) \cdot t}$$

3. Zero Drift (Straight Path)

If we want to land exactly straight across (\(\text{Drift} = 0\)), we must cancel out the river's flow. This means our horizontal velocity component must be exactly equal and opposite to the river's velocity: \(v_{\text{boat}}\sin\theta = -v_{\text{river}}\).

$$\boxed{\sin\theta = \frac{-v_{\text{river}}}{v_{\text{boat}}}}$$

If the river is faster than the boat, this equation yields a number greater than 1, proving it is physically impossible to go straight across!

The formula for radius of curvature is \(R = \frac{v^2}{a_{\perp}}\), where \(a_{\perp}\) is the component of acceleration normal to the velocity vector.

Substituting \(\phi = \theta/2\):

$$\boxed{R = \frac{u^2 \cos^2\theta}{g \cos^3(\theta/2)}}$$

Question 4

A wedge (angle \(\alpha\)) rests on the ground. A particle is projected towards it with velocity u at angle \(\theta\). The wedge accelerates horizontally towards the particle at \(a\). If the particle strikes the wedge exactly perpendicularly, find the time of collision.

Mathematical Solution

In the wedge frame, the wedge is stationary. The particle experiences a "Pseudo-Gravity" due to the wedge's acceleration: \(\vec{g}_{\text{eff}} = \vec{g} - \vec{a}_{\text{wedge}}\).

Since the wedge accelerates towards the particle (leftwards, -x), the pseudo-force is rightwards (+x). Thus \(\vec{g}_{\text{eff}}\) has a downward component \(g\) and a rightward component \(a\).

We resolve the initial velocity and \(\vec{g}_{\text{eff}}\) along the wedge plane (x') and perpendicular to it (y'):

  • \(u_{x'} = u \cos(\theta - \alpha)\)
  • \(u_{y'} = u \sin(\theta - \alpha)\)
  • \(g_{\text{eff}, x'} = g \sin\alpha - a \cos\alpha\) (acting down the plane)
  • \(g_{\text{eff}, y'} = g \cos\alpha + a \sin\alpha\) (acting into the plane)

For a perpendicular strike, the velocity along the plane (\(v_{x'}\)) must be zero when it hits. The time of flight is determined by the y' motion: \(T = \frac{2 u_{y'}}{g_{\text{eff}, y'}}\).

$$v_{x'} = u_{x'} - g_{\text{eff}, x'} T = 0 \implies u_{x'} = g_{\text{eff}, x'} \left( \frac{2 u_{y'}}{g_{\text{eff}, y'}} \right)$$

N identical particles are at the corners of a regular polygon of side a. They move simultaneously with constant speed v, each heading directly towards the next particle. Find the time they meet and total distance traveled.

Mathematical Solution

At any instant, the particles form a regular polygon that rotates and shrinks. Consider Particle 1 and Particle 2. The relative velocity of approach is the component of their velocities directed towards each other.

$$-\frac{ds}{dt} = v - v \cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{N}\right) = v\left[1 - \cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{N}\right)\right]$$

Since the initial distance is \(a\) and the final distance is 0, the time \(T\) taken to meet is:

$$\boxed{T = \frac{a}{v[1 - \cos(2\pi/N)]}}$$

A defense cannon on a flat plain can fire projectiles with a fixed initial speed \(u\), but can tilt its barrel to any launch angle \(\theta\) from \(0^\circ\) to \(180^\circ\). Find the boundary of the region in 2D space that the cannon can defend. In other words, what is the geometric envelope that bounds every single possible trajectory?

Mathematical Solution

The trajectory equation for a given angle \(\theta\) is:

$$y = x \tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2 u^2 \cos^2\theta} = x \tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2 u^2} (1 + \tan^2\theta)$$

To find the envelope (the maximum \(y\) for any given \(x\)), we take the partial derivative with respect to \(\tan\theta\) and set it to zero:

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial (\tan\theta)} = x - \frac{g x^2}{2 u^2} (2 \tan\theta) = 0 \implies \tan\theta = \frac{u^2}{gx}$$

Substituting this back into the trajectory equation gives the bounding envelope:

$$\boxed{y = \frac{u^2}{2g} - \frac{g x^2}{2u^2}}$$

Comprehensive Mathematics

The Lagrangian is \(\mathcal{L} = K - U = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - \frac{1}{2}kx^2\). The Euler-Lagrange equation \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\right) - \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} = 0\) directly gives \(m\ddot{x} + kx = 0\).

Hamiltonian Mechanics

The Hamiltonian is \(\mathcal{H} = K + U = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}kx^2\). Hamilton's equations \(\dot{x} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial p} = \frac{p}{m}\) and \(\dot{p} = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial x} = -kx\) combine to give \(m\ddot{x} = -kx\).

Kinematics of the Double Pendulum

Kinematics means the relations of the parts of the device, without regard to forces. We are only trying to find expressions for the position, velocity, and acceleration in terms of the variables that specify the state of the device.

  • $x$ = horizontal position
  • $y$ = vertical position
  • $\theta$ = angle of pendulum (0 = vertical downwards, counter-clockwise is positive)
  • $L$ = length of rod (constant)

We place the origin at the pivot point of the upper pendulum. The positions $x_1, y_1, x_2, y_2$ in terms of angles $\theta_1, \theta_2$ are:

$$ x_1 = L_1 \sin \theta_1 $$
$$ y_1 = -L_1 \cos \theta_1 $$
$$ x_2 = x_1 + L_2 \sin \theta_2 $$
$$ y_2 = y_1 - L_2 \cos \theta_2 $$

The velocity is the derivative with respect to time:

$$ \dot{x}_1 = \dot{\theta}_1 L_1 \cos \theta_1 $$
$$ \dot{y}_1 = \dot{\theta}_1 L_1 \sin \theta_1 $$
$$ \dot{x}_2 = \dot{x}_1 + \dot{\theta}_2 L_2 \cos \theta_2 $$
$$ \dot{y}_2 = \dot{y}_1 + \dot{\theta}_2 L_2 \sin \theta_2 $$

The acceleration is the second derivative:

$$ \ddot{x}_1 = -\dot{\theta}_1^2 L_1 \sin \theta_1 + \ddot{\theta}_1 L_1 \cos \theta_1 $$
$$ \ddot{y}_1 = \dot{\theta}_1^2 L_1 \cos \theta_1 + \ddot{\theta}_1 L_1 \sin \theta_1 $$
$$ \ddot{x}_2 = \ddot{x}_1 - \dot{\theta}_2^2 L_2 \sin \theta_2 + \ddot{\theta}_2 L_2 \cos \theta_2 $$
$$ \ddot{y}_2 = \ddot{y}_1 + \dot{\theta}_2^2 L_2 \cos \theta_2 + \ddot{\theta}_2 L_2 \sin \theta_2 $$

Forces in the Double Pendulum

We treat the two pendulum masses as point particles. Using Newton's law $F=ma$ on both masses and accounting for tension $T_1$ and $T_2$:

$$ m_1 \ddot{x}_1 = -T_1 \sin \theta_1 + T_2 \sin \theta_2 $$
$$ m_1 \ddot{y}_1 = T_1 \cos \theta_1 - T_2 \cos \theta_2 - m_1 g $$
$$ m_2 \ddot{x}_2 = -T_2 \sin \theta_2 $$
$$ m_2 \ddot{y}_2 = T_2 \cos \theta_2 - m_2 g $$

After eliminating the unknown tensions $T_1$ and $T_2$, we arrive at the exact equations of motion used by our RK4 integrator:

$$ \ddot{\theta}_1 = \frac{-g(2m_1+m_2)\sin\theta_1 - m_2 g \sin(\theta_1 - 2\theta_2) - 2\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)m_2(\dot{\theta}_2^2 L_2 + \dot{\theta}_1^2 L_1 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2))}{L_1(2m_1 + m_2 - m_2\cos(2\theta_1 - 2\theta_2))} $$
$$ \ddot{\theta}_2 = \frac{2\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)(\dot{\theta}_1^2 L_1(m_1+m_2) + g(m_1+m_2)\cos\theta_1 + \dot{\theta}_2^2 L_2 m_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2))}{L_2(2m_1 + m_2 - m_2\cos(2\theta_1 - 2\theta_2))} $$

Lagrangian Mechanics Derivation

The Lagrangian $\mathcal{L} = T - V$ simplifies the derivation by avoiding constraint forces (tension) entirely and using generalized coordinates $\theta_1, \theta_2$.

Step 1: Kinetic Energy ($T$) and Potential Energy ($V$)

The velocities of the masses are found by taking the time derivative of their positions:

$$ v_1^2 = \dot{x}_1^2 + \dot{y}_1^2 = L_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 $$
$$ v_2^2 = \dot{x}_2^2 + \dot{y}_2^2 = L_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 + L_2^2 \dot{\theta}_2^2 + 2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) $$

Substitute these into $T = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_2^2$:

$$ T = \frac{1}{2}(m_1+m_2) L_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 L_2^2 \dot{\theta}_2^2 + m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) $$

The potential energy $V = mgy$ is:

$$ V = -(m_1+m_2)g L_1 \cos\theta_1 - m_2 g L_2 \cos\theta_2 $$

The Lagrangian $\mathcal{L} = T - V$ is therefore:

$$ \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}(m_1+m_2) L_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 L_2^2 \dot{\theta}_2^2 + m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) + (m_1+m_2)g L_1 \cos\theta_1 + m_2 g L_2 \cos\theta_2 $$
Step 2: Euler-Lagrange Equations

Applying the Euler-Lagrange equation for $\theta_1$: $\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_1}\right) - \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_1} = 0$

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_1} = (m_1+m_2)L_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1 + m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) $$
$$ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_1}\right) = (m_1+m_2)L_1^2 \ddot{\theta}_1 + m_2 L_1 L_2 \ddot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) - m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_2 \sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)(\dot{\theta}_1 - \dot{\theta}_2) $$
$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_1} = -m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2 \sin(\theta_1-\theta_2) - (m_1+m_2)g L_1 \sin\theta_1 $$

Combining these terms yields the first equation of motion:

$$ (m_1+m_2)L_1^2 \ddot{\theta}_1 + m_2 L_1 L_2 \ddot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) + m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_2^2 \sin(\theta_1-\theta_2) + (m_1+m_2)g L_1 \sin\theta_1 = 0 $$

Similarly, applying the Euler-Lagrange equation for $\theta_2$ yields the second equation of motion:

$$ m_2 L_2^2 \ddot{\theta}_2 + m_2 L_1 L_2 \ddot{\theta}_1 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) - m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 \sin(\theta_1-\theta_2) + m_2 g L_2 \sin\theta_2 = 0 $$

This system of two second-order ODEs is equivalent to the Newtonian derivation, but achieved purely through scalar energy functions!

Solving this system of linear equations algebraically for $\ddot{\theta}_1$ and $\ddot{\theta}_2$ gives the exact explicit equations of motion used by numerical integrators:

$$ \ddot{\theta}_1 = \frac{-g(2m_1+m_2)\sin\theta_1 - m_2 g \sin(\theta_1 - 2\theta_2) - 2\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)m_2(\dot{\theta}_2^2 L_2 + \dot{\theta}_1^2 L_1 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2))}{L_1(2m_1 + m_2 - m_2\cos(2\theta_1 - 2\theta_2))} $$
$$ \ddot{\theta}_2 = \frac{2\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)(\dot{\theta}_1^2 L_1(m_1+m_2) + g(m_1+m_2)\cos\theta_1 + \dot{\theta}_2^2 L_2 m_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2))}{L_2(2m_1 + m_2 - m_2\cos(2\theta_1 - 2\theta_2))} $$

Hamiltonian Mechanics Derivation

The Hamiltonian framework rewrites the system in terms of generalized positions $\theta_i$ and their conjugate momenta $p_i$, converting the two 2nd-order ODEs into four 1st-order ODEs.

Step 1: Conjugate Momenta

The momenta are derived from the Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}$:

$$ p_1 = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_1} = (m_1+m_2)L_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1 + m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) $$
$$ p_2 = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_2} = m_2 L_1 L_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) + m_2 L_2^2 \dot{\theta}_2 $$

We must invert these relations to express the angular velocities $\dot{\theta}_1$ and $\dot{\theta}_2$ in terms of $p_1$ and $p_2$. This involves a determinant factor $W = L_1^2 L_2^2 m_2 (m_1 + m_2 \sin^2(\theta_1-\theta_2))$:

$$ \dot{\theta}_1 = \frac{L_2 p_1 - L_1 p_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2)}{L_1^2 L_2 [m_1 + m_2 \sin^2(\theta_1-\theta_2)]} $$
$$ \dot{\theta}_2 = \frac{L_1 (m_1+m_2) p_2 - L_2 m_2 p_1 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2)}{L_1 L_2^2 m_2 [m_1 + m_2 \sin^2(\theta_1-\theta_2)]} $$
Step 2: The Hamiltonian ($\mathcal{H}$)

The Hamiltonian is defined as $\mathcal{H} = p_1 \dot{\theta}_1 + p_2 \dot{\theta}_2 - \mathcal{L}$, which represents the total energy $T + V$ expressed entirely in terms of $\theta$ and $p$:

$$ \mathcal{H} = \frac{L_2 p_1^2 + L_1 (m_1+m_2) p_2^2 - 2 m_2 L_1 L_2 p_1 p_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2)}{2 m_2 L_1^2 L_2^2 [m_1 + m_2 \sin^2(\theta_1-\theta_2)]} - (m_1+m_2)g L_1 \cos\theta_1 - m_2 g L_2 \cos\theta_2 $$
Step 3: Hamilton's Equations of Motion

Hamilton's equations are $\dot{\theta}_i = \frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial p_i}$ and $\dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial \theta_i}$. The equations for $\dot{\theta}_1, \dot{\theta}_2$ are already given above. Taking the derivatives for momentum gives:

$$ \dot{p}_1 = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial \theta_1} $$
$$ \dot{p}_2 = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial \theta_2} $$

Computing these partial derivatives yields highly complex algebraic expressions. However, this resulting system of four 1st-order coupled ODEs is the most mathematically elegant way to feed the system into a Runge-Kutta numerical integrator!
